Designing a battery-powered distributed sensing system for a 100 m wind turbine blade.

01 / Executive summary

The honest finding: power budget, not range, is the binding constraint.

After modelling four candidate MCUs across four radio technologies, the analysis converges on a clear winner for the leaf nodes — but also exposes an inconvenient truth: any architecture using Wi-Fi HaLow at every node is incompatible with one-year battery life on practical pack sizes.

Key recommendation

Two-tier architecture: STM32U5 leaf nodes using Si4463 sub-GHz radios within each blade, transmitting via 3-node star topology to a per-blade hub. Hub nodes (also STM32U5) aggregate the three streams and forward over Wi-Fi HaLow down the tower to the base station. This split keeps the high-power HaLow radio off the leaf nodes entirely.

02 / Requirements & data model

What the system must do, and what data it must move.

Each of the 9 sensor nodes inside the blades collects audio at 44.1 kHz alongside IMU data. With a 30-second sampling window every 10 minutes, the duty cycle is approximately 5% — leaving substantial sleep time for energy recovery and a wide transmission window before the next cycle begins.

Why compression matters

Sending raw audio over a low-power radio is the difference between a 15-second transmission and a 6-minute one. OPUS at 32 kbps reduces audio to a manageable ~120 KB per 30-second sample with full intelligibility for structural and acoustic analysis. ADPCM is a lower-CPU alternative at 4:1 compression — worth considering on the SAMD21.

Throughput requirements

Over a 10-minute window, the system must transfer 1.18 MB from inside the blades to the base station. This averages to ~16 kbps system-wide — a trivially small number. The real challenge is not throughput but the burst nature of the transmission and the RF environment it must traverse.

03 / Network topology

Three ways to wire 9 nodes through a rotating composite structure.

The topology choice cascades through every other decision in the system. It determines which radios are viable, how much energy each node spends, and what fails when something breaks.

Rejected

A. Direct star to base

Every node has its own long-range radio to the base station.

  • 9 HaLow radios needed
  • 400 m path through hub structure
  • Catastrophic power cost on leaves
  • Simplest mental model
Recommended

B. Per-blade hub aggregation

Short-range to blade hub, then hub uses HaLow to base.

  • Only 3 HaLow radios needed
  • Leaf hops max 33 m
  • HaLow power penalty isolated to 3 hubs
  • Tower-base link only crosses 300 m once per blade
Alternative

C. Self-healing mesh

Every node can relay for every other node.

  • Redundant paths if one node fails
  • Higher idle current — periodic listening
  • Significant firmware complexity
  • Standardised via Bluetooth Mesh

Within a blade: three sub-topology options

Inside each blade, the three nodes can talk to the hub via a star, a daisy chain, or a mesh. The star wins on simplicity, energy, and link margin when paired with a sub-GHz radio.

04 / RF propagation

The blade interior is hostile to 2.4 GHz radio.

A modern wind turbine blade is a composite shell of fibreglass and carbon fibre over a foam or balsa core. Carbon fibre, in particular, behaves as a partial Faraday cage at 2.4 GHz — attenuating signal by 20–40 dB through a single layer. Sub-GHz frequencies penetrate this same material with 10–20 dB less loss.

Important caveat

These calculations are based on free-space path loss plus typical published carbon fibre attenuation figures. Actual values depend on specific blade construction (manufacturer, layup, spar geometry). For any real deployment, an RF propagation survey inside a representative blade is essential — both with the blade stationary and rotating, since multipath behaviour changes dynamically.

Si4463 at +13 dBm — the verified pick

Per Si4463 datasheet Rev 1.4 [1]: TX current 44.5 mA at +13 dBm, RX current 13.7 mA, sensitivity -103 dBm at 500 kbps. Combined with 915 MHz sub-GHz penetration through composite blade material, this gives 116 dB total link budget. After path loss and antenna/spar losses, ~50 dB margin remains at the 33 m intra-blade hop — well above the 20 dB industrial standard.

Datasheet caveats

Datasheet "typical" values assume specific test conditions: 3.3 V supply, +25 °C, optimal matching network. Real applications may see 10–20% higher current draw due to PCB matching losses, antenna mismatch, and temperature variation. The published nRF5340 IC has a hardware max of +3 dBm without an external PA; +8 dBm operation requires an external front-end such as the nRF21540. Validate against bench measurements before committing to battery sizing.

Why HaLow only at the hub

Wi-Fi HaLow's 200–300 mA TX current is incompatible with one-year battery life if deployed at every node. By restricting HaLow to the 3 blade hubs, we accept a higher power burden on those 3 devices in exchange for keeping the 9 leaf nodes truly low-power. Hub batteries can be physically larger because the hub enclosure sits at the blade root with more space and weight tolerance.

06 / MCU selection

The cost of choosing a friendly toolchain.

The most popular MCU for IoT prototyping — ESP32-S3 — is also the most energy-hungry by an order of magnitude. The honest analysis shows STM32U5 and nRF5340 essentially tied on power, with STM32U5 winning slightly on raw efficiency and the nRF5340 winning on integrated BLE convenience.

The recommendation: STM32U5

The STM32U5 wins on three independent criteria that all matter for this application: sleep current is 5–10× better than alternatives, wake time is essentially instantaneous (eliminating boot-energy waste every cycle), and the development ecosystem is mature and well-documented through STM32CubeIDE. The lack of integrated radio is a feature in this context, not a bug — you pair it with the right radio for the link rather than being locked into BLE.

Cold weather derating

LiPo batteries lose roughly 30% of usable capacity at –20°C. For deployments in cold climates, multiply the recommended pack size by 1.4×, or use Li-SOCl₂ (lithium thionyl chloride) primary cells which maintain capacity down to –55°C. Li-SOCl₂ has higher energy density (~700 Wh/kg vs 250 Wh/kg) but is non-rechargeable.

Sensitivity to cycle interval

Doubling the cycle interval roughly doubles battery life, since most of the energy is spent on active operations (sampling, compression, TX) rather than sleep. This is the single biggest knob you have.

08 / Full system architecture

Putting it all together.

The final architecture is a two-tier hierarchy: 9 ultra-low-power leaf nodes inside the blades, 3 medium-power hub nodes at the blade roots, and 1 mains-powered base station at the tower foot. Sub-GHz radios handle intra-blade communication; Wi-Fi HaLow handles the tower descent.

Complete two-tier system architecture. Nine leaf nodes feed three blade hubs via sub-GHz star topology. Three hubs feed the base station via Wi-Fi HaLow down the tower.

System timing diagram — one 10-minute cycle

t = 0 s │ All 9 leaf nodes wake simultaneously, begin sampling t = 30 s │ Sampling complete, begin OPUS compression (parallel) t = 60 s │ Compression complete, leaves prepare for TX t = 60–73 s │ Blade 1: nodes 1A, 1B, 1C transmit to Hub 1 (TDMA, 4 s each) t = 73–86 s │ Blade 2: nodes 2A, 2B, 2C transmit to Hub 2 t = 86–99 s │ Blade 3: nodes 3A, 3B, 3C transmit to Hub 3 t = 99 s │ All leaves sleep. Hubs prepare HaLow link. t = 99–144s │ Hubs transmit aggregated data sequentially over HaLow t = 144 s │ Base station has all data. Hubs sleep. t = 600 s │ Next cycle wake — hub broadcasts time sync beacon

Total active time: ~144 seconds out of 600 (24% active for hubs, ~16% for leaves including sleep gaps). The remaining ~7.5 minutes is available as margin for retries, weather, and clock drift between cycles.

09 / Practical concerns

The wind turbine perspective — vibration, rotation, weather, access.

A wind turbine is one of the most hostile environments engineered devices regularly survive. The blade tip moves at up to 300 km/h, the structure flexes continuously, temperature swings 60°C between seasons, and physical access requires climbing or specialised rope work. The design must account for all of this.

Rotation effects on the RF link

EffectMagnitudeMitigation
Doppler shift ~25 Hz @ 915 MHz Negligible — well within carrier tolerance
Multipath variation Continuous FEC coding, automatic retry with backoff
Tower body blocking ~3 s per rotation Buffer + retry; never align TX with tower pass
Antenna orientation ±360° Circularly-polarised antenna or dual orthogonal monopoles
Centrifugal force at tip ~20 g sustained Conformal coating, captive fasteners, no socketed components

Mechanical & environmental design checklist

Enclosure & mounting

  • IP67 minimum (dust-tight, submerge-rated)
  • UV-stable ABS or polycarbonate
  • Adhesive structural bond, not screws into composite
  • Hold-down strap as redundant retention
  • Total mass < 500 g per node to minimise centrifugal load

Electrical & PCB

  • Conformal coat (acrylic or urethane) — vibration + humidity
  • All connectors locking or soldered (Molex Micro-Lock, JST-GH)
  • No through-hole sockets; only surface-mount
  • Battery secured mechanically, not just electrically
  • TVS diodes on all I/O — lightning is a real concern at altitude

Antenna placement

  • Mount on inner trailing-edge wall (away from CFRP spar)
  • 15 mm minimum standoff from any conductive material
  • Hub antenna: external chip antenna on dedicated keepout PCB area
  • HaLow antenna at blade root pointing down the tower
  • Optional: external short whip on hub for HaLow link

Time synchronisation

  • GPS at base station provides UTC reference
  • Hub broadcasts 1 ms sync beacon at cycle start
  • Crystal drift < 10 ppm = 6 ms over 10 min
  • 500 ms advertising window accommodates worst-case drift
  • Cross-blade timestamp accuracy: ~100 µs achievable

Reliability and recovery strategy

The single biggest robustness investment is store-and-forward: every node has local flash or microSD storage holding at least 7 days of compressed data. If any link fails — radio, hub, base, or backhaul — data persists locally and re-transmits on the next successful cycle. The system tolerates entire days of communication outage without data loss.

Failure scenarios & outcomes

Leaf node radio fails: Data accumulates locally up to 7 days, alarms via missing heartbeat. Hub fails: All 3 leaves accumulate locally; replacement hub can be deployed without data loss. HaLow link fails: Hub buffers up to 2 days of aggregated data. Base station fails: System continues collecting; data delivered when base recovers. Battery depleted: Last 7 days of data accessible via direct USB/SD retrieval during maintenance.

Energy harvesting supplement (optional)

A vibration-powered piezoelectric harvester mounted on the blade can supplement the battery budget. Typical output in a wind turbine blade is 5–50 mW continuous depending on placement and wind conditions. Even a conservative 20 mW average provides ~1,700 J/day — covering roughly 15% of a leaf node's energy budget and extending pack life by approximately the same fraction.

Verification procedure

To independently verify any datasheet value referenced here:

  1. Identify the radio in question (e.g. "Si4463 at +13 dBm")
  2. Open the corresponding datasheet from the bibliography above
  3. Navigate to the Electrical Characteristics or Power Consumption section (usually §4 or §5)
  4. Find the row matching the operating mode and TX power level
  5. Compare the typical value to the figure in this report — they should match within ±10%
Why typical ± 10%?

Datasheet "typical" figures are measured under specific test conditions (usually 3.3 V supply, 25 °C, optimal matching network). Real implementations deviate due to PCB matching losses, antenna mismatch, supply voltage variation, and temperature. Production designs should validate against bench measurements on the actual hardware rather than relying solely on datasheet typicals.
